### Why are these rules the way they are?

You see, while AI seems smart or wonderful at first, and it can be, it's probably the worst decision anyone can make when working on a big or useful codebase. It is actively detrimental to a real human's skill at understanding code and coding. When someone falls into the illusion of AI, they become victims to the following two effects: the Dunning-Kruger effect and/or the Gell-Mann amnesia effect. The dunning Kruger effect applies mostly to someone who doesn't know how to code, aka a vibe coder. These will turn into delusional AI-lovers and get defensive when their unmaintainable garbage slop code is called unmaintainable garbage slop code. And hey, I get it, I used to vibe code some things myself as well. But it was miserable and shameful to know that I couldn't code without the help of AI anymore. When I picked up coding by myself again, it turned out to be much more fun than just stupid vibe coding. The other effect is the Gell-Mann amnesia effect, showing why AI can't be easily trusted. If someone knows a lot of coding and finds a bunch of bugs in AI-generated code, but then doesn't realize errors in other subjects, then... yeah they don't realize they have too verify that too. It can be the other way around with people who don't know to code either. Essentially, vibe coding creates instant gratification but cripples coding ability and codebase stability in the long term. So please, don't do it.
